01 Executive Summary

The thesis, in three sentences

Duolingo produces measurable receptive-skill gains but not conversational competence, and the gap is a property of the system — not the learner. Across fourteen sources spanning cognitive psychology, second-language acquisition, human-factors engineering, and first-party algorithmic disclosure, the holes align across all four HFACS layers: the operator has no signal, the environment has no context, the supervisor measures the wrong variable, and the organization never measures what matters. Our proposed intervention — dialect-specific conversational AI agents — targets the three preconditions the literature says are required for transfer: episodic context, social embodiment, and sociolinguistic variation.

This dashboard organizes the research we've gathered against the five-phase final-project rubric, traces the HFACS chain through the evidence, and is honest about what the literature supports and what it doesn't. The goal is to make every claim we plan to make on a slide traceable back to a source on this page.

Duolingo's measured ceiling

One-week vocabulary retention: ~92% when each language was learned in its own distinct VR context vs. ~76% in a uniform context. Episodic encoding matters. (Essoe et al., 2022 — paraphrased figures)
d = 1.3
Personalization Principle
Effect size across 10 of 10 experiments: conversational delivery produces deeper learning than formal delivery. The persona isn't decoration — it's the mechanism. (Mayer, 2021)
d = 0.58

Meta-analysis across 17 effectiveness studies (k = 100 effect sizes): conversational-agent practice produces a medium effect on L2 proficiency. Intervention-side evidence. (Bibauw et al., 2022)

04 The HFACS Chain — Holes Aligned

The class gave us the method: work backward through all four HFACS levels, avoid blaming individuals, look for system breakdowns. The Swiss Cheese tells the story — holes at every layer, and they line up.

Level 1 · Unsafe Acts
Skill-Based Errors with No Signal
Learners practice routines that produce consistent wrong outputs. Signal Detection Theory explains why: the correct signal (speaking competence) is never placed in the operator's attentional field. What's visible — XP, streaks, crowns — is a False Alarm.
Evidence: Jiang (2021); Kristensen (2024); Kornell & Bjork (2008)
Level 2 · Preconditions
Episodic Buffer Foreclosed by Design
Working memory's Episodic Buffer binds verbal content to context. Duolingo engages only the Phonological Loop. Separate contexts produce ~92% retention vs. ~76% in uniform contexts — a mechanism the gamified interface architecturally forecloses.
Evidence: Baddeley (2000); Essoe (2022); Godwin-Jones (2018); Mihaylova (2022); Cannon-Bowers & Salas (1998)
Level 3 · Unsafe Supervision
Algorithm Optimizes the Wrong Variable
Duolingo's Birdbrain, by the company's own description, matches item difficulty to learner ability — an item-correctness optimizer. It does not detect, adapt to, or feedback on conversational performance. Supervisor d′ for transfer failure is effectively zero.
Evidence: Bicknell & Brust (2020); Jiang (2021); Kristensen (2024); Godwin-Jones (2018); Cannon-Bowers & Salas (1998)
Level 4 · Organizational
DAU Incentives Select Against Transfer
The Innovator's Dilemma in motion: engagement KPIs (DAU, streaks) reward exactly the features that forecast poorly for transfer. Transparency about the wrong signal — confident progress indicators — amplifies a mis-calibrated learner model rather than surfacing the gap.
Evidence: Mayer (2021); Bhaskara (2021); Jiang (2021); Godwin-Jones (2018)

Reading the chain upward: organizational KPIs exclude speaking (L4) → the algorithm never tasks or measures speaking (L3) → the environment provides no contextual encoding (L2) → the learner has no signal to act on (L1). The incident — failure to transfer to real conversation — is structurally predicted. This is the course's core tenet made concrete: error is a system property, not a human shortcoming.

05 Name the Enemy

Following the class framing — Rosling, Tufte, Musk — we name each failure directly. Four enemies, four sourced mechanisms.

01
The Decontextualized Drill
Vocabulary taught as isolated items, not in socially embedded context. Spaced repetition works for memorization; it does not build pragmatic knowledge. Baddeley supplies the mechanism — the Episodic Buffer that binds content to context — and Essoe supplies the behavioral evidence that distinct encoding contexts lift retention.
Godwin-Jones · Baddeley · Essoe
02
The Wrong Signal
Streaks, XP, and crown levels are transparent — about the wrong variable. TADMUS and automation-transparency research both show that confident displays of the wrong metric can degrade performance relative to diagnostic feedback, because they suppress the skepticism that would prompt supplementary practice.
Cannon-Bowers · Bhaskara · Kornell & Bjork
03
The Receptive-Only Evidence Base
Duolingo's own outcome research measures reading and listening. Speaking is unsupervised by design. Birdbrain, by the company's own description, optimizes item-correctness, not conversational competence. The supervisor's detection sensitivity for the project's terminal goal is zero.
Jiang · Bicknell & Brust
04
The Aligned Holes
The Swiss Cheese stack — L4 KPIs, L3 algorithm, L2 environment, L1 learner — all open in the same place. Any single hole could be worked around; all four open together makes failure structural. The redesign target is any layer; changing one misaligns the stack.
Reason's framework · our full corpus
06 Intervention Evidence — Why a Conversational Agent

The three pillars of the redesign — each sourced

Our proof-of-concept rests on three claims the literature supports. We're not inventing new mechanisms; we're combining three well-evidenced ones that gamified apps have left on the table.

Pillar 1 · Dialogue Works
Bibauw et al. (2022), a three-level meta-analysis across 17 studies, found a medium effect (d = 0.58) of dialogue-based CALL on L2 proficiency — with stronger effects for system-guided interaction and corrective feedback. Our agent scaffolds, not just responds.
Pillar 2 · Persona Matters
Mayer (2021) — personalization d = 1.3 across 10/10 experiments; the Voice and Embodiment Principles add further, separate effects. A Mexican-Spanish "Carlos" and a Castilian-Spanish "Elena" aren't UX decoration — conversational style plus embodiment is what invokes Social Agency Theory.
Pillar 3 · Dialect Is the Input
Geeslin & Long (2014) — communicative competence requires acquiring sociolinguistic variation, not a standardized idealization. Dialect-specific agents put real input variability in front of the learner, which the classroom and the app both omit.
An honest note. Wang et al. (2024) ran a 12-week quasi-experiment with adult learners (n = 99, CEFR B1–B2) and found that an embodied GenAI chatbot significantly improved willingness to communicate, self-perceived competence, and reduced speaking anxiety — but did not move pre-to-post measured speaking performance. Our reading: affective and motivational preconditions move fast; objective speaking scores need longer or more structured intervention. A proof-of-concept is proof of plausibility, not proof of effect. Proposing a future RCT is the right move.

08 Gap Analysis — What the Research Doesn't Yet Support

The rubric asks for a gap analysis in Phase 5. Here are the honest holes in our evidence base — the places a skeptical reader should push.

KNOWN
Kristensen (2024) is second-graders, not adults
The error-repetition study population is 363 Norwegian L1 children, not adult Spanish learners. The mechanism likely generalizes, but we use it for mechanism-level argument only, not as direct adult-learner evidence. Paired with Mihaylova and our adult-facing sources on the intervention side.
KNOWN
Wang (2024) is WTC and anxiety, not speaking performance
The one adult-learner GenAI-chatbot study in the corpus shows affective gains but no measurable speaking-performance lift in 12 weeks. We're explicit about this in the intervention section rather than hiding it behind the positive findings.
MISSING
No primary data of our own
Given the two-week timeline we didn't run a user study, which means we can't make causal claims about our specific proof-of-concept. Our Phase 3 methodology is research-and-build; Phase 5 proposes a future RCT against a control group.
MISSING
No direct evidence that dialect-specific agents outperform a single-dialect agent
Geeslin & Long supports the theoretical case that sociolinguistic variation is acquisition-relevant, but we don't have an A/B evaluation of dialect-specific vs. generic Spanish agents. This is a clear candidate for the future-study proposal.
NEXT
Eye-tracking could sharpen the Level 1 attention claim
The IX Lab eye-tracking option from the rubric could turn the inattentional-blindness argument from conceptual to measurable: where does learner gaze actually fixate during a Duolingo session vs. during a conversation with our agent? A follow-on study worth naming in the lightning talk.
