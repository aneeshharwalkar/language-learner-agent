## Summary

- Scholarly textbook from Routledge's Second Language Acquisition Research Series synthesizing variationist sociolinguistics with SLA theory; widely adopted in graduate Hispanic linguistics and SLA courses, co-authored by Kimberly Geeslin (Professor of Hispanic Linguistics, Indiana University).
- Argues that communicative competence in a second language requires acquiring **sociolinguistic and dialectal variation** — not merely a single standardized form — because native-speaker input is variable along regional, register, and social-group dimensions that shape grammaticality judgments in real interaction.
- Reviews empirical evidence that L2 learners systematically underproduce regionally marked forms even when their grammatical competence is high, producing the "bookish" register gap familiar to advanced classroom learners — a direct analogue of Duolingo's transfer gap.
- Frames the classroom-vs.-native-speaker variation gap as a **pedagogical design problem**, not a learner deficit: if instruction presents only a standardized idealization, learners cannot acquire the variability that actual conversation requires.
- Supplies the theoretical basis for this project's core design choice — distinct **Mexican-Spanish** and **Castilian-Spanish** agents rather than a single "Spanish" agent. Dialect-specific input is not a niche feature; it is what the variationist SLA literature identifies as missing from standardized classroom and app curricula.
