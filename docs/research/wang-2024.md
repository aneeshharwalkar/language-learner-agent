# Wang et al. (2024) — Conversational GenAI Chatbots and Adult EFL Speaking

**Citation (APA):**
Wang, C., Zou, B., Du, Y., & Wang, Z. (2024). The impact of different conversational generative AI chatbots on EFL learners: An analysis of willingness to communicate, foreign language speaking anxiety, and self-perceived communicative competence. *System*, *127*, 103533. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.system.2024.103533

---

## Summary

- Quasi-experimental study with 99 Chinese undergraduate EFL learners (ages 19–23, CEFR B1–B2), randomly assigned across a control group and two experimental groups using a text-only GenAI chatbot or an embodied avatar-based chatbot (D-ID Agent), over a 12-week intervention.
- The avatar-based (embodied) chatbot group showed **significant improvements** in willingness to communicate (WTC) and self-perceived communicative competence (SPCC), and a **significant reduction** in foreign language speaking anxiety (FLSA), compared with controls.
- Critically: **pre- and post-test speaking performance showed no significant differences across groups.** Affective and self-perception gains were real; measurable speaking-performance gains within a 12-week window were not.
- This is exactly the kind of honest, mixed result the HFACS argument benefits from acknowledging: AI conversation partners are plausibly *necessary* for conversational transfer (they shift the affective and motivational preconditions), but a short intervention is **not sufficient** to move objective speaking scores — suggesting duration, integration, and instructional scaffolding all matter.
- The embodiment advantage observed here converges with Mayer's (2021) Embodiment Principle: on-screen agents with social cues produce deeper engagement than disembodied ones.
