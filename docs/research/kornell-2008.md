# Kornell & Bjork (2008) — Metacognitive Illusions in Self-Directed Study

**Citation (APA):**
Kornell, N., & Bjork, R. A. (2008). Learning concepts and categories: Is spacing the "enemy of induction"? *Psychological Science*, *19*(6), 585–592. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02127.x

---

## Summary

- Participants studied paintings by 12 artists under blocked (massed) or interleaved (spaced) schedules, then identified the artist of novel paintings — a test of inductive category learning rather than rote recall.
- Interleaving produced substantially better inductive performance than blocking — the opposite of participants' predictions before the test and the opposite of participants' judgments *even after* taking the test.
- This is a documented **metacognitive illusion**: learners systematically misjudge which study schedule is working. Subjective fluency during blocked practice is interpreted as learning, even when transfer performance shows it was not.
- Directly applicable to Duolingo's streak/XP feedback loop: subjective fluency cues (correct answers in the moment, streak continuation, crown completion) are exactly the signals Kornell & Bjork show are *unreliable* indicators of durable learning. Learners' confidence in their progress is not evidence of transfer.
- Supports the HFACS Level 1 "False Alarm" argument: within-session success signals generate a confident "present" response to a learning signal that is not in fact there — this is a property of human metacognition, not a learner failing.

**Key Quote:**
> "Even after testing, and contrary to their own test performance, participants rated massing as more effective than spacing."

---

**Rubric Phase:** Phase 1 — supplies the cognitive-psychology basis for the claim that learner overconfidence in app-based progress is a predictable metacognitive illusion, reinforcing the Level 1 argument that the False Alarm is a system-induced signal-detection failure rather than a motivational one.
